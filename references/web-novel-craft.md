## 1. Story Engine

Treat a story as purpose, form, materials, and drive:

- **Purpose**: theme, emotional target, genre promise, and reader expectation.
- **Form**: macro structure, arc, act, chapter, scene, and hook.
- **Materials**: character, setting, action, dialogue, narration, detail, clue, symbol.
- **Drive**: desire, goal, conflict, stakes, pressure, reversal, consequence.

Before expanding any idea, identify:

- what the protagonist wants
- why they cannot get it
- what price failure carries
- what pleasure the reader expects
- what question remains unresolved after each chapter

## 2. Main-Brain Chapter Pipeline

Default chapter pipeline:

```text
user ideas / suggestions / prior context
-> relevant skills and references
-> chapter task card
-> Draft A
-> user feedback
-> Draft B
-> user approval
-> Draft C de-AI pass
-> continuity record
```

Keep the stages separate:

- Draft A writes the chapter.
- Draft B fixes user-directed content issues.
- Draft C removes AI flavor and naturalizes prose after content is approved.

## 3. Idea Expansion

Do not wait for a perfect idea. Start from an ordinary idea and push it through contradictions.

Expansion sequence:

1. State the plain idea.
2. Pick a genre promise.
3. Choose a protagonist with a lack, wound, desire, or unfair situation.
4. Add an obstacle that directly blocks the desire.
5. Add stakes: what is lost if they fail?
6. Add a twist or pressure source.
7. Add a relationship that complicates action.
8. Escalate three times.
9. Decide the climax choice.
10. Define the ending state.

When blocked, ask:

- What would make this more urgent?
- What would make the protagonist personally responsible?
- What would make the solution create a new problem?
- What detail makes the idea visible in a scene?

## 4. Character and Plot

Balance character and plot. Plot-only stories feel like moving pieces; character-only stories can lose momentum.

A usable character card should include:

- role in the story
- external goal
- internal need or wound
- core trait
- fear, shame, or false belief
- relationship pressure
- speech style
- first impression
- key choice under pressure
- likely change by the end of the arc

Plot integration test:

- If this character is removed, does the plot change?
- If this event is removed, does the character still change?
- Does every major plot turn force a character choice?
- Does every major character choice change the external situation?

## 5. Conflict and Suspense

Conflict is any pressure between desire and obstruction.

Types of conflict:

- external opponent
- social rule or institution
- scarcity and time pressure
- moral dilemma
- relationship contradiction
- inner fear, guilt, pride, obsession, or wound
- hidden information

Suspense tools:

- information gap
- dramatic irony
- deadline
- delayed reveal
- interrupted success
- cliffhanger
- false victory
- consequence after action
- dialogue subtext
- scene-level reversal

Each scene should contain at least one unresolved question. Each chapter should close with changed stakes, not merely stopped action.

## 6. Scene and Chapter Beats

Use this scene beat skeleton:

```text
Setup: where are we, who is present, what pressure already exists?
Goal: what does the viewpoint character want right now?
Obstacle: who or what blocks them?
Action: what do they try?
Turn: what new information, failure, cost, or reversal appears?
Aftermath: what emotion or decision follows?
Hook: what question pulls the next scene/chapter?
```

For serial web novels:

- give each chapter a visible mini-goal
- change the situation before the chapter ends
- keep paragraphing readable
- avoid solving every question at once
- let small wins create larger trouble
- keep recurring desire visible so the reader remembers why the story matters

## 7. Draft A/B/C Standards

### Draft A

Purpose: write a complete chapter based on context and the task card.

Quality gate:

- continues from prior context
- contains a clear chapter goal
- has conflict and situation change
- keeps canon intact
- ends with a hook or handoff

### Draft B

Purpose: revise content according to user feedback.

Quality gate:

- user feedback is classified and addressed
- approved parts are preserved
- local plot, details, reactions, and conflict are improved
- no new contradictions are introduced

### Draft C

Purpose: final de-AI and prose naturalization pass after user approval.

Quality gate:

- plot and canon are preserved
- generic language is removed
- emotions are embodied in action and detail
- dialogue sounds character-driven
- paragraph rhythm feels less mechanical

## 8. De-AI Polishing

Common AI flavor:

- stock phrases
- over-explained emotions
- generic atmosphere
- symmetrical paragraphing
- summary sentences at emotional peaks
- dialogue that exists to explain plot
- everyone speaks too clearly
- polished but bloodless descriptions

Fixes:

- replace abstract emotion with gesture, action, silence, and sensory detail
- cut repeated explanation
- add specific lived-in objects and context
- let characters evade, interrupt, misunderstand, or leave things unsaid
- vary sentence and paragraph length
- end scenes on concrete change instead of moral summary
- keep a little roughness when it fits the character

Draft C should not introduce major new plot events unless the user requests it.

## 9. Continuity Records

After finalizing a chapter, record:

```text
Final chapter events:
New canon:
Character changes:
Relationship changes:
Foreshadowing added:
Foreshadowing resolved:
Unresolved questions:
Next-chapter handoff:
Forbidden contradictions:
```

For long projects, maintain:

- story bible
- character ledger
- timeline
- location and setting rules
- foreshadowing table
- chapter records
- style preferences

## 10. Revision Checklists

First chapter checklist:

- protagonist is identifiable
- situation is clear
- immediate pressure appears
- genre promise is visible
- goal or forced choice appears
- exposition is minimized
- concrete details carry meaning
- ending creates a next-chapter question

Outline checklist:

- protagonist desire is strong enough for a long story
- antagonist/obstacle system can escalate
- each arc changes status, power, relationship, or knowledge
- stakes grow, not merely repeat
- side plots pressure the main plot
- climax requires a costly choice

Scene checklist:

- viewpoint character wants something
- opposition exists
- action changes the situation
- emotion follows from consequence
- the scene could not be deleted without loss

Prose checklist:

- paragraphs are readable for web format
- actions are clear
- dialogue has purpose and voice
- important emotion is dramatized
- worldbuilding is attached to action
- repeated explanation is cut

AI draft checklist:

- matches outline
- preserves continuity
- avoids generic filler
- keeps protagonist agency
- includes sensory and behavioral details
- ends with a hook or changed stakes

## 11. Additional Local Tutorial Lessons

These notes come from rereading the local B站教程 and writing-craft ebooks.

### Ordinary Idea Expansion

An idea does not need to be brilliant at first. Make it story-worthy by adding five forces:

```text
protagonist -> goal -> obstacle -> price -> why now
```

Practical test:

- Is the protagonist readable, sympathetic, interesting, or under pressure?
- What concrete thing do they want right now?
- Why can they not solve it easily?
- What bad result happens if they fail?
- Why must this scene begin at this exact moment?

If a scene feels flat, increase the price or add a second obligation that complicates the original goal.

### Opening Detail

Strong openings often use small concrete details to imply large conditions. A detail should do work:

- reveal scarcity, power, humiliation, desire, danger, obsession, or contradiction
- imply backstory without explaining it
- establish viewpoint distance
- create a reader question

Avoid opening with abstract labels when a visible action can carry the same meaning.

### Early Web-Novel Chapters

Early chapters should quickly clarify:

- who the protagonist is
- what situation they are in
- what immediate pressure appears
- what the reader is being promised
- what conflict makes the chapter move
- why the next chapter is needed

Do not overload the first three chapters with every setting rule. The reader first needs orientation, pressure, and a reason to continue.

### Character Wound to Plot

For character-driven plotting, use:

```text
core trait -> wound / knot -> pressure -> wrong choice -> consequence -> key moment -> changed choice
```

The character's inner knot should not stay private. Externalize it through actions, relationships, failures, and turning points.

### Conflict and Suspense

Conflict is the pressure system. Suspense is the timing system.

Use conflict through:

- external opposition
- inner contradiction
- relationship pressure
- social rules
- scarcity
- time limits
- moral dilemma
- dialogue subtext

Use suspense by controlling:

- what the protagonist knows
- what the reader knows
- when consequences arrive
- when success is interrupted
- when a reveal is delayed
- what question remains after the scene

### AI Long-Form Production

Do not ask AI to create a long novel in one pass. Use layered production:

```text
story bible
-> volume outline
-> unit outline
-> chapter task card
-> chapter beats
-> Draft A
-> structural/content review
-> Draft B
-> de-AI Draft C
-> continuity record
```

The most important AI guardrail is selective context: provide the relevant bible, prior chapter handoff, chapter task card, and forbidden changes instead of dumping everything.

Before writing an important chapter, create or update a chapter blueprint and key-information file:


```text

不会说什么 / 不会做什么：
主线之外的生活痕迹：
如何自然撞上主线：
```

### Living Character Test

A character is not alive just because they have a label. Test them through:

- independent goal
- information boundary
- profession or class habit
- relationship pressure
- private fear or desire
- ordinary obligation
- speech limits
- action under pressure

If the character only appears to explain clues, move the protagonist, or deliver a hook, rewrite the scene.

### Information Boundary Test

Characters must not act like they know the author's outline.

- A police officer should suspect realistic causes first: lies, conflict, public safety, prior relationship, mental state, accident, motive, evidence gap.
- A doctor should notice injuries, shock, delayed responses, symptoms, medical procedures, patient boundaries.
- A family member should first respond as family: panic, disbelief, unfinished calls, objects, practical questions.
- A passerby should provide partial, biased, incomplete testimony.

### Detail Multi-Function Test

Important details should do at least two jobs:

- make the place real
- expose a character habit
- become evidence
- pressure a relationship
- foreshadow a consequence
- echo theme
- change meaning when repeated

Details should be filtered through POV. A system planner, doctor, police officer, and family member should not notice the same things in the same order.

### Case-Derived Applications

Use `excellent-novel-case-methods.md` for the detailed case study. Practical condensed lessons:

- From `活着`: ordinary unfinished life makes death heavier than abstract grief.
- From `白夜行`: indirect evidence can shape characters more powerfully than self-explanation.
- From `福尔摩斯`: clues must be observable, interpretable, testable, and later recoverable.
- From `三体`: large concepts need human pressure points and concrete institutions.
- From `1984`: systems become believable through daily restrictions and tiny private acts.
- From `动物农场`: even simplified group characters need clear social function and desire.
- From `哈利波特`: wonder works best when embedded in repeatable daily systems.
- From `百年孤独`: repeated details can change meaning across time and generations.
- From `飘`: strong desire makes characters active, flawed, and readable.
- From `房思琪的初恋乐园`: trauma and pain must respect character boundaries and should not be used as mere plot fuel.
- From `卡拉马佐夫兄弟`: family scenes can become moral trials when every person speaks from desire, guilt, pride, belief, and self-defense.
- From `明朝那些事儿`: complex background becomes readable when translated into concrete problems, interests, choices, reversals, and stakes.
- From `呐喊`: short fiction can make a whole world visible through compressed scenes, ordinary speech, irony, and one memorable object or sentence.
- From `撒哈拉的故事`: unfamiliar settings become alive through lived actions, transactions, mistakes, neighbors, food, paperwork, and the narrator's eye.
- From `杀死一只知更鸟`: limited viewpoint can make readers experience moral growth instead of receiving moral explanation.
- From `失踪的孩子`: long relationships should contain mixed emotions, dependency, rivalry, love, resentment, and self-justifying memory.
- From `天龙八部`: multi-protagonist serials need independent desire lines that cross and change identity, alliance, and moral position.
- From `小王子三部曲`: symbolic objects should carry loneliness, distance, affection, and choice instead of becoming decorative metaphors.
- From `新海诚套装`: time, memory, body-swap, weather, and city imagery work only when tied to concrete emotional loss and pursuit.
- From the `32部欧美文学不朽名作` collection: use anthologies as mechanism libraries: trial, revenge, misunderstanding, downfall, redemption, pursuit, social pressure, and disillusionment.
