## What The Batch Says In Writing Terms

### 1. Opening first, prose second

The opening must do real commercial work fast. The title, synopsis, first scene, protagonist, current crisis, and future direction should all be visible early.

What this means for my fiction:

- The first chapter must clearly answer: who is this, what went wrong, what is the hook, and why continue now?
- The first 6000 words are not decoration; they are the screening window.
- Three things should be obvious early: protagonist identity, central contradiction, and immediate pressure.

### 2. A premise needs a visible contradiction

A good hook is not only a concept. It is a concept with friction: success and loss, identity and shame, power and constraint, desire and obstacle.

What this means for my fiction:

- If the premise can be summarized in one line, make sure that line contains a conflict, not just a setting.
- The premise should create a question the reader wants answered in scene form.
- A synopsis should not hide the core contradiction behind vague language.

### 3. Systems and golden fingers need a reward loop

System-based stories work because small actions produce disproportionate rewards. The loop must be simple, visible, and easy to anticipate.

What this means for my fiction:

- Give the protagonist a clear action -> reward path.
- Let the reward escalate in size, visibility, and consequence.
- The system should not only reward; it should also create new pressure, new status, or new complication.

### 4. Long arcs need nested goals

For long commercial arcs, the writer should set the opening hook and climax first, then break the middle into smaller goals and intermediate bursts.

What this means for my fiction:

- Use one large promise, then split it into smaller chapter goals.
- Each 5k-20k segment should have its own mini payoff.
- A 100k arc should not feel like one endless straight line; it should have multiple converging lines.

### 5. Fan fiction should not copy the original road map

Good fan fiction is not a recitation of the original. It should cut in from a fresh entry point and tell an independent story inside the known world.

What this means for my fiction:

- Do not write "the same plot again."
- Choose a fresh angle, a different starting point, or a different protagonist position.
- Use familiarity for attraction, but use novelty for retention.

### 6. Daily scenes need contrast, not water

Daily-life writing becomes memorable when character mismatch, hidden intent, or expectation reversal is present.

What this means for my fiction:

- A daily scene should still change relationship pressure, status, or emotional temperature.
- The comedy or charm should come from contrast: looks vs behavior, image vs reality, role vs action.
- If a scene only records routine, it is not doing its job.

### 7. Plot boxes are reusable

The writer should collect reusable story boxes such as apprenticeship, acquiring power, joining a group, uncovering a secret, or solving a local problem.

What this means for my fiction:

- Break a large idea into a series of small, executable story boxes.
- Each box should have a goal, an obstacle, a result, and a next target.
- Good plotting is often the recombination of familiar boxes with new motivations and settings.

### 8. Classic Chinese works are training material, not costumes

The `1092606875-1-208.mp4_原文.docx` transcript also reinforces a useful reading method: learn from Chinese classics and high-level genre works with a specific target. The point is not to worship "classic prose" vaguely, but to collect how classic stories solve recurring story boxes such as first love, apprenticeship, gaining power, entering a group, meeting a mentor, or causing a later problem through an earlier choice.

What this means for my fiction:

- Read classics with a writing question, not with empty admiration. For example: how does this work introduce a mentor, turn a meeting into attachment, make dialogue reveal hierarchy, or make a small choice become later trouble?
- Use Chinese classics to train sentence rhythm, scene compression, dialogue taste, and character entrance, but do not copy archaic diction, famous phrases, or the recognizable voice of a specific author.
- A useful exercise is "same story box, many versions": collect 20-100 versions of apprenticeship, first encounter, gaining power, revenge trigger, public humiliation, secret organization entry, or rescue scene, then extract the mechanism.
- Deliberate imitation should be low-risk practice, such as short fanfic-style exercises or rewritten scene mechanics, not publishable copying.
- Use comparison to build self-knowledge. If an exercise exposes the gap between my prose and a master, that is useful feedback, not failure.

## Practical Writing Workflow

When using this batch for a new chapter or new book:

1. Write the premise in one sentence.
2. Mark the protagonist's current crisis.
3. Identify the one reward loop, one hidden contradiction, and one delayed payoff.
4. Split the first 6000 words into scene tasks.
5. If the story is long, set a burst point around a major milestone and build nested subgoals toward it.
6. For daily scenes, force contrast or reversal.
7. For fan fiction, choose a fresh cut-in point and write an independent story line.
8. For weak plots, decompose the idea into story boxes before drafting.
